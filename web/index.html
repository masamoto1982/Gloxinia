What am I looking at?

The network sees two residues a, b ∈ {0…p−1} and must output (a + b) mod p. It only ever trains on a fraction of all pairs, so the data underdetermines the rule: a lookup table that memorizes the training pairs fits just as well as the true additive structure.

  • One-hot (opaque): residues are an arbitrary basis, the group structure is hidden. The net memorizes first — train accuracy hits 100% while validation stays at chance and val loss rises. Then weight decay slowly shrinks the high-norm memorized table until the low-norm structural circuit becomes cheaper, and validation snaps to 100% in a late, visible step. That step is grokking.
  • Weight decay = 0: the engine is off. The net memorizes and never groks — val accuracy stays flat forever while the weight norm runs away.
  • Fourier (transparent): nearby residues map to nearby points on a circle, so the group structure is exposed in the input geometry. The sharp memorize-then-snap is gone — the net learns diffusely instead of grokking. The drama of grokking is a property of the medium, not of the concept being learned. (At this small, fast p the transparent arm learns partially; the clean "generalizes with no step" result lives in the larger p=97 regime in the research notes.)

This is the repository's minimal_grok.py experiment, ported to Rust and compiled to WebAssembly so it runs entirely on your machine — no server, no network. See the source and research notes.
